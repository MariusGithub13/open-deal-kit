# I · The Prompt Library

**100+ copy-paste prompts, one for every moment in a deal.**

Copy, paste, and put Claude to work. Each prompt is written to keep Claude honest: state only what the documents support, cite the source, and say "not stated" rather than guess. The one rule runs through all of them, *every factual claim is cited back to the page it came from, or it gets cut.*

## Thesis, sourcing & outreach
*8 prompts*

1. Turn my rough idea into a sharp, testable acquisition thesis, with must-haves and disqualifiers.
2. From my thesis, list the sub-sectors and business traits worth targeting, and where to find candidates.
3. Build me a simple buy-box one-pager I can screen inbound deals against.
4. Draft a warm, owner-direct outreach message that doesn't read like a broker. Brackets for specifics.
5. Write two follow-up messages that each add a reason to reply rather than just bumping.
6. Draft the note that moves a first conversation toward a teaser or basic financials.
7. Write the NDA-request message that makes it easy for an owner to say yes.
8. Given this list of targets, help me prioritise who to approach first, and why.

## Reading a CIM
*20 prompts*

9. Summarise this CIM in one page, plain English, and cite the page or section behind every figure.
10. List every financial figure in this CIM with the exact page or table it appears on. Flag any number stated without a source.
11. What is this business actually selling, to whom, and how does it make money? Answer only from the CIM and quote the lines.
12. Identify every claim that reads as marketing rather than verifiable fact, and what evidence would confirm each.
13. Find every use of "adjusted", "normalised", "pro forma" or "run-rate" and explain what's being adjusted and why it may not survive a sale.
14. List the five most important things this CIM does NOT tell me, the gaps a careful buyer would notice.
15. Where do two figures or statements in this CIM contradict each other? Quote both, with page numbers.
16. Rewrite the "investment highlights" stripping every adjective, leaving only verifiable facts. What's left?
17. Based only on this CIM, what are the three things most likely to kill this deal in diligence?
18. Pull out every forward-looking projection and note what it rests on. Which assumptions are doing the heavy lifting?
19. Describe the revenue model precisely: recurring vs one-off, contracted vs at-will, citing where the CIM supports each point.
20. If I could ask the seller only five questions after this CIM, what should they be and why?
21. What's the seller's likely reason for selling, from the CIM's framing, and what would I verify about it?
22. Take the single most impressive number in this CIM and stress-test it: what must be true for it to be real and durable?
23. Give me a one-paragraph bull case and a one-paragraph bear case, each grounded in cited facts.
24. List the people-and-owner dependencies this CIM reveals, and what happens if each person leaves.
25. What does this CIM tell me about customer concentration, and what does it carefully avoid telling me?
26. Map this CIM against a standard diligence checklist. Which areas are well-covered, which are thin or absent?
27. Give me a "first 24 hours" list: the highest-value things to verify before I invest more time.
28. Turn this CIM into a half-page screening memo a busy buyer could decide from, cited throughout.

## Reading the financials (P&L / balance sheet)
*19 prompts*

29. Walk through this P&L line by line and tell me, plainly, what each trend means. Quote the figures.
30. Is revenue growing, flat or declining, and steady or lumpy? Show the year-on-year numbers.
31. What's happening to gross and operating margins over the period, and what could explain the movement?
32. List every EBITDA add-back and rate each "legitimate", "arguable" or "aggressive", with reasoning.
33. Reconstruct owner-independent EBITDA by removing add-backs that won't survive a change of control. Show your working.
34. What working-capital pattern does this business have, and how much cash would I need to fund a year of it?
35. Identify any one-off or non-recurring items propping up profit in any year.
36. What does the balance sheet say about debt, leases and obligations I'd be assuming?
37. Are there signs of deferred capex or aging assets, a bill coming due that the projections ignore?
38. Compare the revenue trend to the cost/headcount trend. Is profit coming from growth or from cutting?
39. Which three numbers here would I most want an accountant to verify independently, and why?
40. Flag any figure that contradicts a figure stated elsewhere in the materials.
41. Does reported profit actually turn into cash? Point to the evidence.
42. Find revenue labelled "recurring" and tell me whether the financials actually support the label.
43. If current margin trends continued two more years, what would this business look like?
44. Build me a quick quality-of-earnings red-flag list from these financials.
45. What questions should I put to the seller's accountant based on these statements?
46. Translate these financials into the five things a lender would care about.
47. Give the plain-English version of this business's financial health in five sentences, each tied to a figure.

## Reading a teaser / one-pager
*10 prompts*

48. Read this teaser as a skeptical buyer. What's actually being sold, in two sentences?
49. List the hard facts (revenue, EBITDA, sector, location, terms) and mark anything vague or merely implied.
50. What's conspicuously missing, the things sellers leave out when they aren't flattering?
51. Given my thesis (pasted), is this worth pursuing? A quick fit read and a reason.
52. What three questions would tell me fast whether this is worth a real look?
53. What does the framing suggest about the seller's motivation and sophistication?
54. Red-flag scan this teaser: anything hinting at trouble underneath?
55. Draft the reply that requests the next level of information without overcommitting.
56. Score this teaser against my buy-box out of 10, with reasoning.
57. What would this business plausibly be asking, and what supports or undercuts that guess? Label it a guess.

## Working a data room
*10 prompts*

58. Here's the data-room index, which documents should I read first to confirm or kill the deal fastest?
59. What standard diligence documents are missing from this index, by category?
60. Cross-check these documents against the CIM's claims and flag every discrepancy.
61. From these contracts, extract the change-of-control, termination and exclusivity clauses that matter to a buyer.
62. Summarise each customer contract's term, value, renewal and termination rights in a table.
63. Read these employment agreements and flag key-person, non-compete and retention risks.
64. What in this data room raises a new question or contradicts what I was told earlier?
65. Extract every liability, contingent liability or off-balance-sheet obligation across these documents.
66. Give me a running open-items list: received, outstanding, not yet requested.
67. Build a prioritised reading order based on where the biggest risks usually hide.

## Spotting red flags
*13 prompts*

68. Scan everything for customer-concentration risk and quote the evidence.
69. Scan for owner dependence: how much does the business rely on one person, with proof.
70. Find any sign of revenue-quality problems, pull-forward, one-off spikes, channel stuffing.
71. Identify margin erosion and whether it's a pricing, cost or mix problem, from the data.
72. Flag any add-back that looks designed to inflate the headline EBITDA.
73. Look for single-supplier, single-channel or single-platform dependence.
74. Identify regulatory, licensing or compliance exposure mentioned anywhere.
75. Find where the narrative and the numbers disagree.
76. Give me the worst realistic interpretation of this deal, supported only by cited facts.
77. Rank every risk found by severity and by how cheaply I could verify it.
78. What landmine is most likely buried in the footnotes, and where would I look?
79. Which red flags are dealbreakers and which are negotiable price adjustments?
80. What would a seasoned acquirer be suspicious of here that a first-timer might miss?

## Valuation & price
*11 prompts*

81. What valuation approach fits this business (EBITDA multiple, SDE, revenue) and why?
82. Give a reasoned multiple range for this profile, and what moves it up or down.
83. Calculate the implied multiple at the asking price, rich, fair or cheap? With reasoning.
84. What am I implicitly betting on if I pay this price?
85. Build the case for offering less, grounded in the risks we found.
86. Sketch a rough sources-and-uses at [X]% debt, keep the maths simple and show it.
87. Does cashflow comfortably cover debt service at this price and structure? Show the cushion.
88. Give a conservative / base / optimistic returns sketch and the assumption it's most sensitive to.
89. What structures (earn-out, seller note, deferred) could bridge a price gap?
90. What's the most I could justify paying, and what would have to be true to get there?
91. What price-relevant facts are still unverified, and how would each move the number?

## Questions, meetings & close
*12 prompts*

92. Build the diligence question list from everything so far, grouped by area, marking the deal-critical ones.
93. What five questions are most likely to expose a problem in this specific deal?
94. Prep me for the management meeting: agenda, the questions that matter, and follow-ups for vague answers.
95. What should I watch and listen for, the tells that a problem is being managed around?
96. Turn these red flags into questions I can ask without putting the seller on the defensive.
97. What should I try to see or verify in person on a site visit?
98. Draft the post-meeting follow-up that locks in what was said and chases what's missing.
99. What are my strongest points of leverage in a price negotiation, from the diligence?
100. Draft an indicative offer (LOI) outline grounded in what we've established, brackets for terms.
101. Build the closing checklist by category, flagging the items people forget until late.
102. Turn my diligence into an IC-style memo: opportunity, business, financials (cited), case, risks, valuation, open items, recommendation.
103. Write the one-paragraph verdict on whether this is a deal worth doing, only from what we verified.

---

> *These prompts are analytical tools to speed up your own judgment and help you read deals faster. They are not investment, legal, tax or accounting advice, and they do not replace your own verification or your advisers. Always check the output against the source documents, which is exactly what the cited rule is built to let you do.*
